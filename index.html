What is in my bag?

The Weight of Expectations

The Hidden Expectations

Inside my bag are expectations that feel heavier than anything I physically carry. As the youngest in my family, I am constantly seen as the one with the brightest future ahead. They place their hopes on me—consistent good grades, awards, achievements, excellence in everything I do. At first, their pride felt motivating. But over time, that pride became pressure. The more I achieve, the higher their expectations rise. It never settles. There is no pause, no space to breathe or simply exist without proving something.

Every accomplishment becomes a new standard I must maintain and surpass. I am not only chasing their expectations but also competing against my past self. There are responsibilities attached to their belief in me. Responsibilities to succeed, to represent the family well, to not fail publicly. And when I feel myself slipping even slightly, the fear sets in. The fear of disappointing them, the fear of not living up to the image they proudly speak about. Sometimes it feels like everything I’ve built could collapse if I fail just once.

The Heaviest Pressure

Expectation is the heaviest for they do not end. They only grow. Each achievement raises the bar higher, and I worry that one day I won’t be able to keep up with the version of myself people admire. I fear reaching a point where I can no longer surpass my previous successes, where I may fall behind. The most suffocating part is the possibility of disappointment. Not just theirs, but my own. I am afraid of being stuck in a place where I can no longer “improve,” where I become less than what I was once believed to be.

The Mechanism of Escape

When the weight becomes unbearable, I retreat. I drown myself in fantasy, imagined worlds, stories, scenarios where I am not defined by expectations. It becomes my escape. Since I find it difficult to open up to my family about how overwhelmed I feel, I instead try to silence the chaos in my head with logic. I attempt to reason with myself, constructing practical thoughts to fight the endless “what ifs.” I distract myself with temporary comforts, watching dramas, playing games, consuming stories that allow me to step outside of my own reality. In those moments, I can breathe. Even if it’s only brief, it gives me relief.

1. What are your top three (3) multiple intelligences?

My top three multiple intelligences are Intrapersonal, Verbal/Linguistic, and Bodily/Kinesthetic, and I strongly agree with these results. I have Intrapersonal intelligence because I often spend time understanding my own thoughts and emotions. I like to reflect, write, and figure out why I feel certain things. My Verbal/Linguistic intelligence shows in how I express myself best through writing. I enjoy putting my feelings into words and explaining ideas clearly. Lastly, my Bodily/Kinesthetic intelligence means I learn and connect better through actions and real experiences. I like doing things rather than just talking about them because it helps me understand and feel more connected. These three intelligences truly describe how I think, learn, and express myself.

2. What is your dominant learning style?

Based on the quiz, my dominant learning style is kinesthetic, which means I learn best by doing and experiencing things firsthand. I agree with this result because I understand and remember things more when I’m actively involved rather than just listening or reading. This also reflects how I connect with people, I value actions more than words. Simple things like spending time together, making something, or showing effort through gestures help me feel the connection more deeply. For me, learning and understanding, whether in school or in relationships, happens best when I can see, touch, and do something real, not just talk about it.

3. How will you improve your study habits?

Based on my strengths in Kinesthetic, Intrapersonal, and Linguistic intelligences, I plan to improve my study habits by making learning more active, personal, and meaningful. Since I learn best through movement and hands-on activities, I will rewrite my notes by hand, walk around while reviewing, and teach lessons out loud to help me remember better. My intrapersonal strength helps me understand myself and connect lessons to my own experiences, so I will keep a study journal, set clear goals, and study in a quiet place where I can focus. Lastly, my linguistic intelligence allows me to learn well through words, so I will summarize lessons in my own words, create stories or acronyms to remember terms, and write glossaries of key concepts. By using these strategies together, I can make studying more engaging, effective, and suited to how I naturally learn best.

Summary

Completing the Multiple Intelligences and Learning Styles activity helped me discover more about how I learn and think. I found that my strengths in Intrapersonal, Linguistic, and Kinesthetic intelligences truly describe how I approach both learning and life. I found that everyone learns differently, and there is no single “right” way to study. What matters is knowing yourself and using your strengths to your advantage.
